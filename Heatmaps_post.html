# This piece of code creates a heatmap using the seaborn package in python.
# I also adjusted some of the basic elements to create a visually appealing 
# graph

plt.figure(figsize=(12,3))
sns.heatmap(nr_order_per_month, 
            cmap='Greens',   # set the pallete or color scheme for your plot
            annot=True,      # include the number on the tiles of the heatmap 
            cbar = True,
           fmt='d')    # Include the colorbar on the right 
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Year')
plt.title('Number of orders per Year and Month', color='darkgreen', 
          fontweight='bold', pad = 20)
plt.show()

Here are some conclusions that can be drawn from the heatmap:¶

  1. Yearly Increase in Orders : There's a noticeable yearly increase in the number of orders, particularly in the latter months of the year. For instance, December (Month 12) shows a marked rise from 70 orders in 2003 to 110 in 2004, and a significant spike to 301 orders in 2005.
  2. Seasonal Trends: The data suggests a seasonal trend with peaks typically in the second half of the year. August (Month 8), September (Month 9), and December (Month 12) show consistently high order numbers, with the highest numbers in September for 2004 and 2005.
  3. Data Absence for 2005: From June (Month 6) onward in 2005, the data shows zero orders, which may indicate incomplete data or a stop in operations or reporting for the latter half of that year.
  4. First Quarter Performance: The first quarter of each year (Months 1, 2, and 3) shows a solid performance, with order numbers generally above 50, suggesting steady business in this period across all three years.
  5. Potential Data Issues : The abrupt fall to zero orders in the last six months of 2005 could also point to a potential data collection or entry issue that might need verification.

Heatmaps are a visually compelling way to represent data, and they offer several advantages over traditional tables. Here are three key benefits:

  • **Immediate Pattern Recognition**: Heatmaps use colors to represent data values, making it easier to spot trends, outliers, and patterns at a glance. This visual representation can reveal hidden correlations and distributions that might be less obvious in a table format, where data is just listed numerically.

  • **Enhanced Data Density and Efficiency**: A heatmap can display a large amount of data compactly, allowing viewers to assess a broad dataset in a single view without having to scroll through rows and columns of a table. This makes heatmaps particularly useful for dealing with large datasets, facilitating quick comparisons across multiple variables.

  • **Improved User Engagement**: The use of color gradients not only helps in highlighting differences in data magnitude but also makes the interpretation more intuitive and engaging for users. This can be particularly beneficial in presentations or situations where you need to communicate complex information quickly and effectively to an audience that may not be specialized in data analysis.
